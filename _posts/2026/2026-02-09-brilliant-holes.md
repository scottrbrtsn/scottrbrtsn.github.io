# Brilliant Holes 

## TLDR
Ask yourself the following questions. 

Consider asking your team. 

>Two tests are specified for recognition of the asshole:

>After encountering the person, do people feel oppressed, humiliated, or otherwise worse about themselves?
Does the person target people who are less powerful?

This is a simple test, and anonymous administration of it may yield surprising results. 

## Terms defined: 
- "otherwise worse": can be any level of "lesser than".  This can be nuanced and subtle.  See Liz Wiseman's research below. 
- "target": this does not need be direct and overt bullying, but subversive expression, passive aggressive insults, and belittling of others in a "backbiting" way is also a form of "targeting."
- "powerful": not just positions of social authority.  Especially when dealing with technology, a certain kind of "elitism" is bred in some circles where "my way is the only way."  Therefore they may truly be more "powerful" in some technical sense, but are blind the effect they have on the social aspect of the socio-technical equation.  

## What is the No Asshole Rule?  Where does it come from?

>The No Asshole Rule: Building a Civilized Workplace and Surviving One That Isn't is a book by Stanford professor Robert I. Sutton. 

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_No_Asshole_Rule

## What is the risk in accusing or attempting to solve this problem directly?
- Most likely, these types of individuals of this stature believe everyone else is beneath them. 
- Therefore of they will disagree, will be unwilling to adjust themselves, and all direct attempts to coach or reprimand will simply anger them. 

## Other relevant areas of thought
### [Liz Wiseman and the Multiplier Effect](https://thewisemangroup.com/books/the-multiplier-effect/) discusses a more subtle set of behaviors for all leaders, especially those with the best intentions (i.e. not Assholes, but toxic nonetheless).

She identifies two poles on a spectrum: Multipliers and diminishers. 

>Diminishers—leaders who drain intelligence, energy, and capability from the people around them
Multipliers—leaders who use their intelligence to amplify the smarts and capabilities of the people around them

Assholes might be easier to identify but Wiseman also has a [quiz to identify accidental diminishers](https://thewisemangroup.com/quiz/accidental-diminisher/). 

## Gregor Hohpe and Social Capital. 
These ideas fit perfectly into the conversation of [social capital](https://architectelevator.com/transformation/political-capital/) from Gregor Hohpe.  

> Change isn’t popular, even if people honestly believe that the end result will be positive. For most folks, things get worse before they get better: they need to learn new approaches, may be less productive initially, make more mistakes, or score lower on established performance metrics. Trying to push too much change through an organization will cost you, sometimes your job. A simple model can help you manage the amount of goodwill that you have and how to spend it in a meaningful way.

So if you're driving for change, even if you are 100% correct, your approach may cost you, well, everything.  

## But I'm not an asshole. 

Are you sure?  Consider taking Wiseman's quiz.  Consider the entire list from Professor Sutton.  Maybe you don't have ALL of the characteristics, but nevertheless accidentally diminish the capabilities of those around you.

After preparing this post, I learned more about myself, and fear in some circumstances I may very well be an accidental diminisher.  I believe it doesn't matter how 10x your technical solution is, you are very capable of /10 the people around you. 

## Suggestions to mitigate

- Consider anonymous surveys asking for the two tests.
- Consider coaching a group as to not single out an individual. 
- Oftentimes these types of people are unable to see themselves, and unwilling to adjust course. Therefore simply isolating the problem to minimize impact is appropriate (just like we do for modules in software).
